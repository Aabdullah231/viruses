ORITHOPOX VIRUS

Symptoms

Orthopoxvirus is a genus of viruses in the family Poxviridae and subfamily Chordopoxvirinae. Vertebrates, including mammals and reptiles, and arthropods serve as natural hosts. There are 12 known species in this genus. Diseases associated with this genus include smallpox, cowpox, horsepox, camelpox, and mpox.[1][2] The most widely known member of the genus is Variola virus, which causes smallpox. It was eradicated globally by 1977, through the use of Vaccinia virus as a vaccine. The most recently described species is the Borealpox virus, first isolated in 2015..
Some orthopoxviruses, including the mpox, cowpox, and buffalopox viruses, have the ability to infect non-reservoir species. Others, such as ectromelia and camelpox viruses, are highly host-specific. Vaccinia virus, maintained in vaccine institutes and research laboratories, has a very wide host range. Vaccine-derived vaccinia has been found replicating in the wild in Brazil, where it has caused infections in rodents, cattle, and even humans.Following the eradication of variola virus, camelpox has become one of the most economically important Orthopoxvirus infections, because many subsistence-level nomadic communities depend heavily on camels.Among the path of evolution of the Orthopoxvirus species, many genes are truncated (but still functional), fragmented, or lost. Specialty: Variola virus (smallpox), now eradicated, and the Vaccinia virus, used in the smallpox vaccine.
Symptoms: distinctive rash (flat spots turning into fluid-filled blisters and scabs), fever, swollen lymph nodes, headache, muscle aches, back pain, and fatigue
Usual onset: 1–4 days after exposure
Duration: 2–8 days
Causes: nimal bites, scratches, direct contact with lesions/fluids, contaminated objects, or respiratory droplets
Prevention: Hand washing, flu vaccines
Medication: Antiviral drugs such as tecovirimat (TPOXX), cidofovir, and brincidofovir,
Frequency: 1 billion cases of seasonal orithopoxvirus per year[1]
Deaths: vastly different death rates.
Year:first isolated and identified in the early 1958s

Prevention

Vaccination:

Orthopoxvirus vaccination primarily uses live vaccinia virus-based vaccines—mainly JYNNEOS and ACAM2000—originally for smallpox, which also provide cross-protection against monkeypox (mpox) and other related viruses. They are used for high-risk individuals and post-exposure, often via subcutaneous or intradermal injection, with JYNNEOS being preferred for safety. Primary Vaccines Used: JYNNEOS (MVA-BN): A newer, safer, non-replicating vaccine preferred for most users, including immunocompromised individuals. ACAM2000: A live, replicating vaccinia virus vaccine used for smallpox/orthopox preparedness, with more side effects. Protection Scope: These vaccines provide cross-protection against various orthopoxviruses, including smallpox, mpox (monkeypox), and cowpox. Administration: Given via subcutaneous (under the skin) or intradermal (between skin layers) injection. Who Should Get Vaccinated (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis - PrEP): Laboratory workers handling orthopoxviruses. Healthcare personnel at high risk of exposure. Public health response team members.Post-Exposure Vaccination: Recommended for close contacts of infected individuals to prevent or lessen disease severity. Side Effects: Common reactions include pain, redness, itching, fever, headache, tiredness, and muscle aches. Vaccination is currently targeted rather than routine for the general population, focusing on occupational risk and outbreak response.

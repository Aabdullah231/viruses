FLAVI VIRUS

Symptoms

Flaviviruses are a genus of small, enveloped, positive-strand RNA viruses (family Flaviviridae) primarily transmitted to humans by mosquitoes and ticks. They cause significant diseases including Dengue, Zika, Yellow Fever, West Nile, and Japanese encephalitis. These pathogens utilize arthropod vectors to infect vertebrates, featuring a ~11kb genome that replicates in the host cell cytoplasm.
They are spherical particles, approximately 50 nm in diameter, with a nucleocapsid enclosed in a lipid bilayer studded with envelope (E) proteins. Comprises a single-stranded, positive-sense RNA (ssRNA+) that functions as mRNA upon entering the host cell.Replication occurs in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), creating "replication organelles" that bud into immature particles, which then undergo structural maturation. Primarily arboviruses (arthropod-borne), spread by Aedes and Culex mosquitoes or ticks. Origin: Evolutionary studies suggest the genus emerged roughly 120,000 years ago. Naming & Classification: The genus Flavivirus was formally created in 1974 by the International Committee on Nomenclature of Viruses (ICNV). Etymology: Derived from Latin flavi ("yellow"), honoring the Yellow Fever virus.Key members were discovered throughout the early 20th century, notably Yellow Fever in 1927 and West Nile in 1937.

Complexity

Specialty: transmitted by arthropod vectors (mosquitoes and ticks), making them major arboviruses
Symptoms: Yellow Fever virus, Dengue virus, Zika virus, West Nile virus, Japanese Encephalitis virus.
Usual onset: 1–4 days after exposure
Duration: 2–8 days
Causes: Influenza A, B, and C viruses
Prevention: Hand washing, flu vaccines
Medication: Antiviral drugs such as oseltamivir
Frequency: 1 billion cases of seasonal flavi per year[1]
Deaths: Tens(10) of thousands of deaths yearly
Year:first officially classified in 1974

Prevention

Vaccination:

Preventing flaviviruses like Dengue, Zika, and West Nile primarily involves avoiding mosquito/tick bites through repellents (DEET, picaridin), wearing protective clothing, using bed nets, and installing window screens. Vector control—draining stagnant water and using pesticides—plus vaccination for specific viruses (e.g., Japanese encephalitis) are also key. Use Insect Repellents: Apply EPA-registered repellents containing DEET, picaridin, or IR3535 on exposed skin. Wear Protective Clothing: Cover as much skin as possible with long-sleeved shirts, long pants, hats, and socks. Mosquito Nets & Screens: Sleep under mosquito nets, ideally insecticide-treated, especially during the day for dengue. Use fine-mesh screens on windows, doors, and tents. Avoid Peak Activity Times: Limit outdoor exposure during peak mosquito activity, typically dawn and dusk. Tick Checks: If in tick-prone areas, check body for ticks and remove them promptly. Eliminate Stagnant Water: Drain, cover, or empty containers that hold water (pots, tires, buckets) to stop mosquito breeding. Maintain Water Storage: Ensure rainwater tanks, wells, and septic openings are sealed with fine mesh.

HERPES VIRUS

Symptoms

Herpesvirus previously named and more widely known as Orthoherpesviridae,, is a large family of DNA viruses that cause infections and certain diseases in animals, including humans..
Nine herpesvirus types are known to primarily infect humans, at least five of which are extremely widespread among most human populations, and which cause common diseases: herpes simplex 1 and 2 (HSV-1 and HSV-2, also known as HHV-1 and HHV-2; both of which can cause orolabial and genital herpes), varicella zoster (VZV or HHV-3; the cause of chickenpox and shingles), Epstein–Barr (EBV or HHV-4; implicated in several diseases, including mononucleosis and some cancers), and human cytomegalovirus (HCMV or HHV-5). More than 90% of adults have been infected with at least one of these, and a latent form of the virus remains in almost all humans who have been infected.[8][9][10] Other human herpesviruses are human herpesvirus 6A and 6B (HHV-6A and HHV-6B) and human herpesvirus 7 (HHV-7), which are the etiological agents for Roseola, and HHV-8 (also known as KSHV) which is responsible for causing Kaposi's sarcoma. HHV here stands for "Human Herpesvirus".In total, more than 130 herpesviruses are known, some of them from mammals, birds, fish, reptiles, amphibians, and molluscs. Among the animal herpesviruses are pseudorabies virus causing Aujeszky's disease in pigs, and bovine herpesvirus 1 causing bovine infectious rhinotracheitis and pustular vulvovaginitis.

Complexity

Specialty: lifelong infection, latency, and periodic reactivation.
Symptoms: tingling, itching, or burning sensations, followed by painful, fluid-filled blisters that break into sores on the mouth or genitals.
Usual onset: 1–4 days after exposure
Duration: 2–8 days
Causes: primarily spread through direct skin-to-skin contact, bodily fluids (saliva, genital fluids), or sores from an infected person, mainly via kissing (HSV-1)
Prevention: Hand washing, flu vaccines
Medication: Antiviral drugs acyclovir, valacyclovir, and famciclovir.
Frequency: 1 billion cases of seasonal herpes per year.
Deaths: arely fatal in healthy adults, but it causes significant mortality in newborns (neonatal herpes)
Year:first isolated and identified roughly 5,000 years ago during the Bronze Age,

Prevention

Vaccination:

Substantial” HSV burden demands “crucial” vaccine effortsThere is no approved herpes vaccine (HSV-1 or HSV-2) currently available for the public, but significant research is underway for both preventive (prophylactic) and treatment (therapeutic) options, with several candidates in clinical trials, including mRNA vaccines like Moderna's mRNA-1608, aiming to reduce transmission, disease, and viral shedding. The World Health Organization (WHO) is actively promoting development, recognizing the global burden of herpes, with potential vaccines targeting either prevention of infection or alleviating symptoms in infected individuals, though breakthroughs are still needed. Evolutionary Background: The virus itself is millions of years old, infecting various species long before humans. HSV-1 actually infected hominid ancestors before they split from chimpanzees 6 million years ago. Early Descriptions: Early Greek civilization (approx. 2000 years ago) first used the term "herpes" to describe creeping skin lesions. Modern First Cases: In medical literature, the first reported cases of specific manifestations, such as HSV of the respiratory tract, appeared much later, notably around 1949.

HEPATITIS VIRUS

Symptoms

Viral hepatitis is a group of contagious, inflammation-causing liver infections (A, B, C, D, and E) spread through contaminated food/water (A, E) or blood/body fluids (B, C, D). Symptoms include jaundice, fatigue, and abdominal pain, with risks ranging from acute illness to chronic cirrhosis or cancer. Prevention involves vaccination (A, B) and avoiding exposure; treatments range from antiviral drugs for B and C to supportive care for A.
Types & Causes: Hepatitis A (HAV) and E (HEV) spread via the fecal-oral route. Hepatitis B (HBV), C (HCV), and D (HDV) spread through blood and body fluids. Transmission: A & E: Contaminated food, water, or poor sanitation. B, C, & D: Unsafe injections, needle sharing, unprotected sex, or from mother to child during birth. Symptoms: Many are asymptomatic, but acute symptoms include jaundice, poor appetite, fever, fatigue, and abdominal pain. Treatment: Hepatitis A and E usually resolve on their own. Hepatitis B and C can become chronic and require antiviral medications. Prevention: Vaccines exist for hepatitis A and B. No vaccine is widely available for C, D, or E. Incubation Period: Hepatitis A: Typically 15–50 days. Hepatitis B: Usually 60–90 days, but can range from 45–180 days. Hepatitis C: Usually 2 weeks to 6 months.

Complexity

Specialty:target and cause inflammation of the liver
Symptoms: flu-like illness (fever, fatigue, malaise, joint pain), digestive issues (nausea, vomiting, poor appetite, abdominal pain, pale stools, dark urine), and jaundice (yellow skin/eyes)
Usual onset: 1–4 days after exposure
Duration: 2–8 days
Causes: excessive alcohol consumption, toxins, medications, and autoimmune diseases.
Prevention: Hand washing, flu vaccines
Medication: Antiviral drugs such as oseltamivir
Frequency: 1 billion cases of seasonal hepatitis per year[1]
Deaths: 1.3 million deaths annually,
Year:first isolated and identified in the early 1965

Prevention

Vaccination:

Hepatitis vaccines protect against liver inflammation caused by viruses, primarily Hepatitis B (HepB), which is routine for newborns and recommended for at-risk individuals, preventing lifelong infection and liver cancer; there's also a Hepatitis A (HepA) vaccine for travelers and at-risk groups, offering crucial long-term immunity. The HepB vaccine, given in a series, is highly effective, with the first dose at birth being key to preventing transmission from mother to child. Hepatitis B Vaccine (HepB) Who needs it: All infants (first dose within 24 hours of birth), unvaccinated children, adolescents, adults with risk factors (healthcare workers, diabetics, multiple partners, IV drug users, travelers), and household contacts of infected persons. Schedule: Typically a 3-dose series (0, 1, 6 months), starting at birth, though some vaccines use a 2-dose schedule. Benefits: Prevents chronic infection, reduces liver cancer risk (known as the first "anti-cancer" vaccine), and protects against liver disease. Hepatitis A Vaccine (HepA) Who needs it: Travelers to high-risk areas, men who have sex with men, people with chronic liver disease, and others with increased risk of exposure. Schedule: Usually 2 doses, 6-18 months apart, though it can be given as a combination vaccine (Twinrix, with HepB).
